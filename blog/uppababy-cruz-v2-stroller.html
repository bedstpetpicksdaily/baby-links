Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ The collapse mechanism is genuinely intuitive—two-step fold with a pull strap that actually works without requiring two hands and a prayer. This isn't a minor feature when you're trying to wrangle a toddler while loading groceries into a sedan.
  • ✓ Wheel quality exceeds expectations for a compact stroller. The swivel front wheels handle sidewalk cracks and gravel without transmitting every bump directly into the seat, and they lock securely for pushing straight. Real difference in daily usability.
  • ✓ Recline goes nearly flat, which matters more than reviews acknowledge. Your kid can actually nap on longer outings instead of waking up grumpy because the seat angle was too upright. The smooth recline lever doesn't pinch fingers either.
  • ✓ Weighs under 20 pounds without being flimsy. There's a real engineering balance here—light enough to carry in one hand when collapsed, rigid enough that the frame doesn't flex when your 3-year-old throws weight around.
Cons
  • ✗ The canopy coverage is smaller than you'd expect for a stroller at this price point. In July heat, you're realizing pretty quickly that 'UPF 50+' doesn't mean much when the fabric only extends to your baby's chest. Direct sun exposure is still a problem on longer walks.
  • ✗ Storage basket is disappointingly shallow. Fit a diaper bag? Sure. Fit a diaper bag plus any groceries? You're fighting physics. This matters if you're using this as an everyday stroller rather than a travel backup, which some people are considering at this price.

Our Verdict

The UPPAbaby Cruz V2 is a legitimately functional compact stroller that doesn't pretend to be something it's not—and that's actually refreshing. It won't replace a full-size jogger or all-terrain rig, and it's not for parents who prioritize maximum storage or extended shade coverage. But if you need a stroller that collapses without theatrical maneuvering, handles regular urban terrain competently, and doesn't demand you compromise your car's trunk space, it delivers on those specific promises. At its variable price point, you're paying a premium for the engineering and brand reputation rather than a revolutionary feature—which is fair if you value reliability over cost-cutting. July sales are common for summer gear, so if budget is tight, waiting for promotional pricing makes sense. For parents who've dealt with strollers that barely fold or wheels that feel like cardboard, this one genuinely earns its 4.3-star rating.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Cruz V2 compare to the original Cruz?

The V2 updates include a slightly improved wheel suspension, a revised canopy design (though still compact), and a smoother recline mechanism. If you own the original, these aren't must-have upgrades—they're refinements. If you're buying new, the V2 is the version to get. The price difference between them is minimal anyway.

Is this stroller actually lightweight, or is that just marketing?

It's genuinely under 20 pounds without feeling hollow. That said, 'lightweight' is relative—it's lighter than full-size strollers but heavier than umbrella strollers. If you're comparing it to a $80 plastic compact stroller, you'll notice the weight. If you're comparing it to a UPPAbaby Vista, it's noticeably lighter.

Can a newborn use the Cruz V2, or do I need an adapter?

You'll need the UPPAbaby Bassinet or Mesa adapter to use it from birth. The seat alone isn't suitable for newborns. This adds cost if you're planning to use it from day one, so factor that into your budget. For families with older babies or toddlers, this limitation doesn't matter.

Does it work well on uneven sidewalks and rougher terrain?

It handles typical urban sidewalks and light trails reasonably well due to the wheel quality, but it's not an all-terrain stroller. Gravel paths work fine. Serious hiking or sand? You'll feel the limitations. Know your terrain before buying.

How does resale value hold up?

UPPAbaby strollers retain value better than most brands—you can typically resell a Cruz V2 for 60-70% of original price on secondhand platforms. This doesn't make it 'cheap' but it does mean you're not throwing away money if you buy and later decide you need something different.
