Transitioning your baby to a high chair is one of the most exciting milestones in parenthood, but it also introduces a whole new set of feeding challenges. Getting this right from the start—with the proper chair setup and feeding accessories—makes mealtimes safer, cleaner, and more enjoyable for everyone. Since you'll be using your high chair multiple times daily for several years, investing in quality essentials now will save you frustration and money down the road.

What to Look For

  • Easy-to-clean surfaces and removable trays: Babies are messy eaters, and you'll appreciate a high chair with a removable tray that fits in the dishwasher and a wipeable seat with minimal crevices where food gets trapped. Look for smooth plastic or fabric that won't harbor bacteria or dried food particles.
  • Stable, sturdy construction with proper height adjustment: A wobbly chair creates anxiety during feeding and can be dangerous. Choose a high chair with a wide, weighted base and smooth height adjustments so you can position your baby at table level with your other family members—this encourages healthy eating habits and social connection.
  • Comfortable harness and footrest support: A five-point harness keeps your baby safely secured, while a footrest (not just dangling legs) provides crucial support that reduces strain on their developing posture and actually helps them eat better by stabilizing their core.

What to Avoid

  • Chairs with attached, non-removable trays: These seem convenient until you realize you can't wash them properly or slide the chair under your table. You'll end up eating with your baby in isolation rather than as a family, and bacteria builds up in ways that concern even relaxed parents.
  • Lightweight, wobbly designs marketed as "compact": The cheapest high chairs often lack proper base support or use flimsy materials that feel unstable when your baby shifts or leans. This constant micro-anxiety during feeding creates stress for both you and your baby during an activity that should be calm and connective.
  • Complicated multi-position trays or overly elaborate features: More adjustments often mean more things to clean and more ways the equipment can break.
