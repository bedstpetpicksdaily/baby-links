Finding the right stroller for your newborn or infant is one of the most important decisions you'll make as a new parent. A compact stroller that actually fits your lifestyle—whether you're navigating crowded stores, flying to visit family, or squeezing into your car trunk—can make the difference between enjoying outings and dreading them. The best compact stroller for this stage needs to provide safe, comfortable support for tiny bodies while staying light enough to handle with one hand.

What to Look For

  • Full recline capability: Newborns and young infants need to lie completely flat for safe sleep and proper spinal support. Look for strollers that recline all the way to 180 degrees, which is especially important since many babies nap during outings.
  • Weight and folding ease: A truly compact stroller should weigh under 15 pounds and fold to a size you can actually manage one-handed while holding your baby. Test the fold mechanism in the store if possible—it should be intuitive and quick, not require two hands or frustrating contortions.
  • Infant car seat compatibility: For the first 6-12 months, the ability to attach your infant car seat directly to the stroller frame means you can move your sleeping baby without waking them. Check that your stroller is compatible with major car seat brands before purchasing.

What to Avoid

  • Limited recline options: Avoid strollers marketed as "compact" that only partially recline or require you to add a bassinet attachment for flat-sleeping newborns. This defeats the purpose of compactness and adds weight and bulk you'll actually need to manage.
  • No car seat compatibility: Strollers that don't accept infant car seats might seem lightweight, but they actually create more work and safety concerns during the newborn phase. You'll find yourself struggling with transfers or keeping your baby in an unsafe position longer than necessary.
  • Difficult or two-handed folding mechanisms: A stroller that requires you to use both hands to fold, or requires remembering multiple steps, becomes genuinely frustrating when you're holding a baby, diaper bag, and groceries.
