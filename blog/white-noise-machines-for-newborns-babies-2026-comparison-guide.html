Frequently Asked Questions

Is white noise safe for newborns and babies?

Yes, when used responsibly. White noise is safe for newborns and replicates the womb environment they spent nine months in. However, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends keeping volume at safe levels—typically under 50 decibels for continuous use. All the machines reviewed here offer adjustable volume controls to prevent hearing damage. Avoid placing speakers directly in the crib; position them at least 3-7 feet away.

How loud should a white noise machine be for a baby?

The FDA and AAP recommend keeping white noise under 50 decibels for safe, continuous exposure near a baby's ears. Most quality machines allow you to adjust volume to appropriate levels. Start at lower volumes and increase only if needed to mask household noise. Remember: the goal is to mask disruptive sounds, not to create maximum volume.

Can white noise machines help with colic or sleep regression?

White noise can help soothe colicky babies by providing a consistent audio backdrop that mimics the womb and may help reduce startle reflexes. For sleep regression, white noise maintains a predictable sleep environment during developmental changes. However, white noise alone won't resolve underlying colic—it's one tool among many. Consult your pediatrician if colic or severe sleep regression persists.

Will my baby become dependent on white noise?

Babies can become accustomed to white noise as a sleep cue, but dependency isn't inevitable. Most experts recommend establishing white noise as part of a consistent bedtime routine rather than the sole sleep requirement. To avoid dependency, gradually reduce volume over time or practice occasional naps and nights without it. Baby Shusher and other models with timer functions make this transition easier.

How long should I use a white noise machine for my baby?

You can use white noise throughout infancy and into toddlerhood. Some parents use it until age 3-4, while others continue longer. There's no strict age limit, but many experts suggest gradually transitioning away as your child grows—using it for naps first, then transitioning to nighttime only, then discontinuing. Start planning this transition around age 2-3 if you want to eliminate dependency.

What's the difference between white noise and brown noise?

White noise is a consistent, crisp sound at all frequencies. Brown noise (or red noise) is deeper and lower-pitched, with more bass. Brown noise sounds like a gentle rumble or distant thunder and is often described as more soothing for older children and adults. White noise is typically preferred for newborns because it better mimics womb sounds. Machines like LectroFan High Fidelity offer both, letting you experiment as your baby grows.

Can I use a white noise app instead of a physical machine?

Apps can work, but dedicated machines are generally safer for nurseries. Machines keep screens out of the sleep environment (reducing blue light exposure), don't require a phone by the crib, eliminate distractions, and use less battery/power. That said, portable options like Yogasleep Rohm are affordable enough that a dedicated device is typically a better investment than relying on your phone.

Should I use white noise for naps and nighttime sleep?

Yes, consistency helps. Using white noise for both naps and nighttime creates a reliable sleep cue that signals to your baby it's time to rest. This consistency can actually help your baby sleep better in both contexts. Once your baby is older, you can transition to using it selectively (nighttime only, for example) if desired.
