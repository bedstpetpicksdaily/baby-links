What to Look For

  • BPA-free, borosilicate glass construction: Look for bottles made from medical-grade borosilicate glass rather than standard glass. Borosilicate glass doesn't leach chemicals when heated, and it resists thermal shock—meaning you can safely move bottles from warm water to cold storage without cracking. This stability matters for babies with sensitivities, as you'll often be warming bottles or preparing them ahead of time.
  • Soft, slow-flow nipples in natural rubber or medical-grade silicone: Sensitive stomachs are often paired with sensitive feeding reflexes. A nipple that mimics breastfeeding and delivers milk slowly helps your baby pace their intake, which reduces gas, spit-up, and digestive upset. Avoid standard silicone nipples that may contain manufacturing residues or latex-containing rubber that could trigger reactions.
  • Anti-colic venting systems: Glass bottles paired with proper venting (like a vent tube or specially designed air circulation) prevent your baby from swallowing excess air during feeds. Less air means less gas and bloating—a genuine concern for babies with sensitive digestive systems.

    Are glass baby bottles safe for babies with sensitive stomachs?

    Glass bottles themselves don't directly reduce gas, but they work well when paired with anti-colic nipples that slow milk flow and reduce air intake.     Glass is non-porous and won't absorb odors, stains, or bacteria like plastic can, which is crucial for sensitive babies.
