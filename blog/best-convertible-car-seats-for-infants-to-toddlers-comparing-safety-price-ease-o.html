Frequently Asked Questions

Can I really use the same convertible car seat from newborn to age 8?

Yes, but with limitations. Extended-range convertible seats accommodate rear-facing from 5–50 pounds and forward-facing to 65 pounds, theoretically covering birth through age 8. However, most children transition to booster seats by age 6–7 for comfort and fit reasons, not safety requirements. The seats are technically usable for that span, but you may choose to transition earlier based on your child's size or preference.

What does 'extended rear-facing' actually mean, and why does it matter?

Extended rear-facing means the seat supports rear-facing use to 45–50 pounds instead of the older standard of 35 pounds. Crash data shows rear-facing protects children's fragile necks and spines far better during accidents. The AAP recommends keeping children rear-facing as long as possible. Extended capability means your toddler can stay rear-facing into preschool years, maximizing that protection window during ages when accidents are most common.

How do I know if a convertible car seat will actually fit in my car?

Check your vehicle's owner manual for LATCH anchor locations, and measure your car's seating depth. Most convertible seats are 28–32 inches wide, which fits standard vehicle seats. Wider vehicles have fewer fit issues. Both LATCH installation and seat belt installation are options on modern convertibles, giving you flexibility if one method doesn't work in your vehicle. The Graco Extend2Fit and Cosco Scenera work with both, providing options if one fails.

What's the actual difference between 'easy to install' marketing claims?

Marketing uses 'easy' loosely. Real differences: tool-free systems vs. those requiring tools; color-coded belt paths vs. unmarked; built-in level indicators vs. guesswork; consistent harness systems throughout vs. learning two different adjustment methods. The Graco SlimFit explicitly includes all four features. The Extend2Fit focuses on the no-rethread harness and LATCH flexibility. Read detailed reviews and watch installation videos before buying based on 'easy installation' alone.

Is there a real safety difference between a $100 Cosco seat and a $200 Graco seat?

Both meet federal safety standards (FMVSS 213), so catastrophic crash protection is equivalent. The differences are in extras: the Graco options offer EPS side-impact foam, extended rear-facing to 50 pounds vs. lower limits, and RightSpan legroom expansion. These are genuine upgrades that improve comfort and some injury prevention, but the Cosco is not 'unsafe.' The Graco options provide better protection and features; the Cosco provides adequate protection at half the price. Your budget tolerance determines which trade-off makes sense.
