Finding the right stroller for a newborn who will travel frequently is one of the most important decisions you'll make in those early months. An all-terrain stroller needs to handle everything from city sidewalks to gravel paths, while still providing the safety and comfort your newborn requires. When travel is part of your lifestyle, you need a stroller that doesn't compromise on either performance or portability.

What to Look For

  • Robust Suspension and Wheel System: Look for strollers with independently moving wheels and shock absorption that can tackle uneven surfaces, gravel, sand, and rough terrain without jostling your newborn. All-terrain wheels should be at least 7 inches in diameter, with locking front wheels for stability on challenging ground.
  • Lie-Flat Capability for Newborns: Your newborn needs a fully reclined position for proper development and safety during the first months of life. Ensure the stroller has a completely flat seat or accepts a compatible bassinet insert, and verify that your baby can remain safely positioned without any incline.
  • Travel-Friendly Weight and Fold: An all-terrain stroller will naturally be heavier than lightweight options, but look for models under 30 pounds that fold compactly enough to fit in car trunks or airplane overhead bins. Quick-fold mechanisms and included travel bags are essential for frequent travelers.

What to Avoid

  • Lightweight "Travel" Strollers with Limited Recline: These compact options might fold small, but they typically only recline partially, which isn't suitable for newborns who need a completely flat sleeping surface. You'll end up needing both a travel stroller and a proper newborn stroller, defeating the purpose of consolidation.
  • Air-Filled Pneumatic Tires: While they provide excellent suspension, pneumatic tires require maintenance and can puncture—a real problem when you're traveling far from home. For your peace of mind during travel, stick with foam or solid rubber all-terrain wheels.
  • Strollers Over 35 Pounds: Bulky all-terrain strollers become unwieldy when you're navigating airports, train stations, or storing them in rental car trunks. Combined with a travel-weary newborn and diaper bag, excessive weight will exhaust you quickly.
