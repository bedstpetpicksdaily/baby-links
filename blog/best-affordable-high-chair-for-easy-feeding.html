What to Avoid

  • Chairs with Fabric Seat Pads: While they look nice and feel cushiony, fabric seats trap food particles and become breeding grounds for bacteria. Unless you're willing to remove and wash padded covers after every single meal, stick with wipeable plastic or vinyl options.
  • Ultra-Lightweight Chairs: Some budget models sacrifice stability to keep costs down. If a high chair wobbles when your toddler moves around or seems like it might tip if they lean forward enthusiastically, it's a false economy—safety issues aren't worth the savings.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the best cheap high chair that's easy to clean?

Look for high chairs with removable, dishwasher-safe trays and wipeable seat cushions made from PU leather or vinyl rather than fabric. Avoid models with lots of crevices and hard-to-reach corners, as they trap food debris. Brands like IKEA Antilop and Graco Contaminant offer budget-friendly options with minimal assembly and maximum cleanability.

How much should I spend on a high chair for a baby?

You can find quality, functional high chairs for $50-$150 that will last through multiple children. Basic models without fancy features (like adjustable height or reclining seats) are often just as effective for feeding and typically cost $50-$100. Premium features add cost but aren't necessary—focus on stability, easy cleaning, and proper height for your table instead.

What features matter most in an affordable high chair?

Prioritize a sturdy, wide base for safety, an adjustable tray that removes easily, and a seat that's the right height for your dining table. Easy-to-clean materials, simple design (fewer parts break), and a secure harness are more important than extras like wheels or vibration features. Test the tray release mechanism in stores—some budget models have stiff, difficult-to-use trays.

Is it worth buying a budget high chair or should I spend more?

Budget high chairs ($50-$100) work just fine if they're stable and easy to clean—these are the most important factors for feeding. You only truly need expensive features if you plan multiple uses (like portable feeding, reclining positions, or fancy materials), which most parents don't actually use regularly. Save your money on an affordable model and invest it elsewhere in baby gear that gets more daily use.
