    Frequently Asked Questions

    How long can a baby stay in a rear-facing car seat?

    Most convertible rear-facing car seats can be used from birth until your child reaches the seat's weight or height limit, typically between 40-50 pounds or 49-50 inches tall. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends keeping children rear-facing until at least age 2 or until they reach the maximum height and weight for their seat. Some extended rear-facing seats allow children to remain rear-facing until age 3 or 4.

    What's the difference between a rear-facing infant seat and a convertible car seat?

    Infant car seats are designed specifically for newborns and typically work rear-facing only until about 12-15 months or 30 pounds, then you need to switch to a new seat. Convertible car seats can be used rear-facing from birth and then converted to forward-facing as your child grows, so they last much longer—often from newborn through booster seat years—making them more economical.

    Can you put a rear-facing car seat in the front seat?

    No, the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) strongly advises against placing any rear-facing car seat in the front seat because the airbag deployment can be dangerous or fatal to an infant. Rear-facing seats must always be installed in the back seat of your vehicle for your child's safety.

    How do I know if my convertible car seat is rear-facing correctly?

    A properly installed rear-facing seat should recline at the correct angle (usually 30-45 degrees) with the seat belt or LATCH connectors tight enough that it doesn't move more than an inch side-to-side or front-to-back. The harness should be snug on your baby's shoulders, with the chest clip at armpit level, and you should not be able to pinch excess webbing. Consult your seat's manual or use a certified child passenger safety technician to verify proper installation.
