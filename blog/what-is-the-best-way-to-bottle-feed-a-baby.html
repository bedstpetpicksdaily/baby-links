The Short Answer

Proper bottle feeding starts with positioning your baby semi-upright in your arms, never laying them flat or propping the bottle. Let your baby lead the feeding by pausing between sucks, watching for hunger and fullness cues, and always burping them halfway through and after feeding. Maintain consistent water temperature for formula, sterilize bottles before first use and after each feeding, and follow safe preparation guidelines to prevent contamination and digestive issues.

The Full Explanation

Bottle feeding your baby correctly requires attention to multiple factors that ensure their nutrition, safety, and emotional connection during feeding time.

Positioning and Hold
Always hold your baby at a 45-degree angle, cradled in your arms with their head supported. This position allows formula to flow naturally to their stomach and reduces the risk of ear infections and aspiration. Never bottle prop or leave your baby unattended with a bottle, as this creates serious safety risks and prevents the bonding that occurs during feeding.

Reading Your Baby's Cues
Babies naturally regulate their own hunger and fullness if given the opportunity. Watch for rooting (turning toward the bottle), active sucking, and hand-to-mouth movements as hunger signs. Signs of fullness include slowed sucking, turning their head away, and closing their mouth. Allowing your baby to control the pace prevents overfeeding and supports healthy eating habits from the start.

Pacing and Burping
Pause every few minutes to allow your baby to catch their breath and pace the feeding naturally. This mimics breastfeeding patterns and reduces gas intake. Burp your baby gently halfway through the feeding and once more after completion by holding them against your chest or across your lap and rubbing their back in slow circles.

Formula Preparation and Bottle Care
Follow your pediatrician's instructions for formula mixing precisely, using water that has been boiled and cooled to room temperature or distilled water, depending on your local water quality. Sterilize bottles before first use by boiling for 5-10 minutes or using a sterilizer. After each feeding, rinse bottles immediately and wash with hot soapy water or run through a dishwasher. Store prepared formula in the refrigerator and use within 24 hours; never leave prepared formula at room temperature for more than 2 hours.

Creating a Feeding Routine
Establish a consistent feeding schedule while remaining flexible for your baby's needs. Newborns typically feed every 2-3 hours, with the amount increasing as they grow. Create a calm feeding environment free from distractions where you can focus on your baby, maintain eye contact, and respond to their communication cues.

What the Experts Say

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) emphasizes that bottle feeding should never involve propping the bottle and recommends parents remain actively engaged during all feedings. Pediatric nutritionists stress the importance of paced bottle feeding—where parents allow the baby to control feeding speed—as research shows this method reduces overfeeding and supports better long-term weight management. Lactation consultants note that proper bottle feeding techniques are especially important for babies transitioning between breast and bottle feeding, as incorrect positioning can lead to nipple confusion and digestive discomfort.

Frequently Asked Questions

What type of bottle is best for bottle feeding a baby?

Anti-colic bottles with angled nipples and venting systems are considered best as they reduce air intake and gas. Look for bottles made from glass or BPA-free plastic, with nipples that match your baby's age and feeding style, whether that's slow-flow for newborns or faster-flow for older babies.

How do I know what bottle nipple size my baby needs?

Newborns typically need slow-flow (Level 1) nipples, which you can graduate from around 3-6 months to medium-flow (Level 2), and then to faster-flow (Level 3) around 6+ months as they grow. Watch for signs your baby is ready to move up, such as taking longer to feed or getting frustrated with milk flow.

Should I sterilize bottles before each use?

For newborns under 3 months, sterilize bottles before first use and after each feeding to prevent infection; after 3 months, washing with hot soapy water is sufficient for most healthy babies. Use a sterilizer, boiling water for 5-10 minutes, or a dishwasher's hot cycle as sterilization methods.

What is the correct bottle feeding position and technique?

Hold your baby at a 45-degree angle (semi-upright) rather than flat to prevent ear infections and choking, and keep the bottle tilted so the nipple stays full of milk. Never prop the bottle, always supervise feeding, and let your baby pace themselves by pausing and checking for hunger cues rather than forcing them to finish.
