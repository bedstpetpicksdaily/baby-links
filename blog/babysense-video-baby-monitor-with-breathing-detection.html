Frequently Asked Questions

Is breathing detection actually accurate, or is it just marketing?

Babysense uses non-contact technology that reads movement patterns rather than direct physiology, which means it's detecting motion consistent with breathing, not actually measuring oxygen levels. It's accurate enough to catch when your baby isn't moving for unusual periods, but it can flag false positives during sleep position changes. Think of it as a sophisticated motion detector, not a medical monitor. Parents report it's useful for catching actual problems, but you'll want to calibrate your expectations if you're hoping for clinical-grade accuracy.

How does this compare to WiFi-only monitors like Nanit or Owlet?

Babysense includes a separate handheld unit that works without WiFi, which is a real advantage if your home network is spotty or if you prefer not streaming video to the cloud constantly. Nanit and Owlet are more app-focused and feature-heavy, but they cost significantly more. Babysense trades some app sophistication for a more traditional monitor experience with breathing detection added. If WiFi reliability matters to you (especially in July heat when routers can struggle), the dual-mode setup gives you a backup.

Will the false alerts keep me awake more than they help?

This depends entirely on your anxiety baseline and your baby's sleep patterns. Parents with highly active sleepers report more false alerts. If your baby tends toward stillness, you'll see fewer pings. The real question: will extra alerts reassure you or stress you? Some parents disable notifications after the first week. Others find the alerts help them catch genuine issues. Read through actual owner reviews to see if other parents' sleep situations match yours before buying.
