Most babies are ready to start learning cup drinking between 6-9 months old, though many won't master it until 12-18 months. The transition typically happens gradually, starting with sippy cups and progressing to open cups as your child develops the necessary coordination and muscle control.

The Short Answer

You can introduce a cup around 6 months when your baby shows developmental readiness signs like sitting up with minimal support and showing interest in what you're drinking. Most pediatricians recommend offering a cup during meals while continuing bottle or breastfeeding, making the transition gradual rather than abrupt. By 12 months, many babies can drink from a sippy cup with handles, though some take longer. The key is following your baby's lead rather than forcing a specific timeline.

The Full Explanation

The transition from bottle to cup is a developmental milestone that varies significantly from baby to baby. Unlike sleep training or other parenting benchmarks, there's genuine flexibility in cup introduction timing, which means you can work with your child's unique readiness rather than against a rigid schedule.

Developmental Readiness Signs at 6 Months

Around 6 months, babies typically develop the oral motor skills needed for cup drinking. Look for these indicators: your baby can sit upright with minimal support, shows interest in what others are drinking, reaches for objects deliberately, and can move food or liquid from the front to the back of their mouth. Not every baby will show all these signs simultaneously, and that's completely normal.

The 6-9 Month Introduction Phase

If your baby shows readiness signs, you can start offering water or breast milk in an open cup or sippy cup during meals. Keep expectations low—most 6-month-olds will be messy and may only consume small amounts. This phase is more about familiarity and learning the mechanics of cup drinking than actual nutrition. Offering a cup once or twice daily during a meal is sufficient; don't stress if your baby shows no interest yet.

The 9-12 Month Transition Period

Between 9-12 months, most babies develop better hand-eye coordination and can hold a sippy cup with handles. This is when many parents actively encourage cup use during meals. Your baby might still prefer bottles for comfort or convenience, and that's acceptable. Some parents offer bottles for bedtime and milk, while introducing cups for water and meals. This mixed approach works well for many families.

The 12-18 Month Progression

By 12-18 months, many children can drink from a sippy cup with reasonable success, though spills are inevitable and normal. Open cups with handles are the next step, typically mastered between 15-24 months. Some toddlers are ready earlier; others take longer. There's no developmental concern if your 18-month-old still primarily uses bottles, though most pediatricians encourage weaning from bottles by age 2-3 years.

Why the Gradual Approach Works Better

Abruptly removing bottles can cause stress for both baby and parent. A gradual transition—replacing one bottle with a cup each week or month—allows your baby to adjust while maintaining their preferred feeding methods. This approach also gives you flexibility if your baby is teething, sick, or going through a developmental leap when they might regress to bottles temporarily.
