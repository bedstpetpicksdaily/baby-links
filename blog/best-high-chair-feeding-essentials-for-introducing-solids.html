    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is the best high chair for starting solids?

    Look for a high chair with a wide, flat seat that reclines to at least 45 degrees, as babies need proper support when first eating solids around 6 months. Key features include an easy-to-clean tray, removable seat pad, and sturdy construction that won't tip—brands like Graco, Chicco, and Ingenuity are popular budget-friendly options. Avoid chairs with excessive crevices where food can get trapped, which creates hygiene and choking hazards.

    What feeding utensils do I need for introducing solids?

    Start with soft-tipped infant spoons (not regular metal spoons, which can hurt gums) and opt for spoons with shallow bowls to make scooping puree easier. You'll also want bibs with a catch pocket, a feeding bowl with suction cups to prevent spills, and a cup with handles or a soft spout for drinking practice. Silicone or rubber utensils are safest since they won't break teeth and are temperature-safe for warm foods.

    Do I need a special bib for messy solid feeding?

    Yes, a waterproof or silicone bib with a deep pocket is essential since traditional cloth bibs won't contain the mess of purees and soft foods. Long-sleeved bibs or smocks provide extra protection for clothing, and roll-up bibs are convenient for on-the-go feeding. Avoid bibs with velcro closures, which attract food debris and are hard to clean—opt for snap or buckle closures instead.

    What type of bowls and plates are best for solids?

    Choose shallow, wide-rimmed bowls or plates with suction cups on the bottom to prevent tipping when your baby starts self-feeding. Stainless steel, silicone, or BPA-free plastic works best, as they're durable and easy to clean; avoid anything too deep that makes it hard for baby to scoop with a spoon. Divided plates with compartments are helpful once your baby progresses to multiple foods, keeping flavors separate.
