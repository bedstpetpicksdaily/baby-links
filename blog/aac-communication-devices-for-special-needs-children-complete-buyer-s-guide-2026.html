What Are AAC Communication Devices?

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) devices are tools designed to support or replace speech for individuals who have difficulty communicating verbally. These devices range from simple picture boards to sophisticated tablets with vocabulary software. AAC is not a replacement for speech therapy but rather a complementary tool that enables children to participate in conversations, school activities, and social interactions while they develop or improve their natural speech abilities.

AAC devices fall into two categories: low-tech (picture cards, communication boards) and high-tech (speech-generating devices, apps on tablets). Low-tech solutions are portable, affordable, and require no batteries, making them ideal for travel and multiple environments. High-tech devices offer more vocabulary options and can be customized with voices, making them suitable for complex communication needs.

Key Features to Look for in AAC Devices

When selecting an AAC device for your child, consider vocabulary size and organization, ease of use, portability, customization options, and durability. The best device matches your child's cognitive and physical abilities while remaining intuitive enough for consistent use across home, school, and community settings. Speed of access is crucial—children are more likely to use devices that allow them to communicate quickly and naturally.

Additional considerations include whether the device includes pre-programmed phrases for common situations, supports visual supports like symbols or pictures, and allows parents and therapists to add personalized vocabulary. Integration with other learning tools and compatibility with school-approved systems are also important for seamless communication development.

Frequently Asked Questions

At what age can children start using AAC devices?

Children as young as 18-24 months can begin using low-tech AAC systems like picture boards. High-tech devices can typically be introduced once a child demonstrates basic understanding of cause-and-effect (around 2-3 years), though every child develops differently. Consult your speech-language pathologist for age-appropriate recommendations.

Will AAC devices delay my child's speech development?

No. Research consistently shows that AAC use does not hinder natural speech development. In fact, AAC often facilitates speech development by reducing frustration, improving communication success, and providing motivation for verbal attempts. AAC is a bridge, not a barrier, to speech.

How do sensory toys complement AAC devices?

Sensory toys from brands like Joyrealtoys help children regulate emotions, maintain focus, and build fine motor skills—all crucial for AAC device success. Busy boards develop dexterity needed to navigate touch screens, while sensory tools reduce anxiety that may interfere with communication attempts. Together, they create optimal conditions for learning.

Are AAC devices covered by insurance?

Many insurance plans cover high-tech AAC devices when prescribed by a speech-language pathologist and deemed medically necessary. Coverage varies significantly by plan and location. Check with your insurer and ask your SLP about documentation requirements for reimbursement.

Can my child use multiple AAC devices?

Yes. Many children benefit from using both low-tech systems (portable, quick access) and high-tech devices (complex vocabulary) in different contexts. Some use picture boards at home, tablets at school, and communication apps on family devices—whatever supports successful interaction in each environment.
